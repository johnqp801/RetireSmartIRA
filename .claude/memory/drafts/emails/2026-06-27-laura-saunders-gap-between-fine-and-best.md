## Full sent text

Subject: Re: The gap between fine and best

Laura,

Let me take them in order: what the gap is, where the money leaks, what you need to know to size it, and how that tells you whether to convert.

The gap is the distance between "fine" and "best." McQuarrie is right that the survivor is fine. But two households with the same balance sheet can pay very different lifetime tax depending on when they recognize income. The difference never arrives as a bill you can point to. Nobody gets a statement that reads "you paid $40,000 more than the version of you who planned." It is a counterfactual, which is exactly why it gets ignored.

The money leaks from three places, and they are the same taxes I flagged earlier, now seen as opportunities rather than threats.

IRMAA is a cliff, not a slope. One dollar over a threshold costs the full surcharge for the year. A household that uses Roth conversions to fill brackets in low-income years can avoid crossing a tier in high-income years. The lever is timing, not avoidance.

Traditional balances do not sit still. They compound, and required distributions as a share of the balance rise every year. A household that does nothing in its 60s lets the account grow into a larger forced-withdrawal problem at 73 and beyond. The pre-RMD window is when you have the most control and the least mandatory income.

The transitions are predictable. When one spouse dies, the same income is taxed as single, and the first IRMAA threshold drops from $218,000 to $109,000. A couple sitting safely under the joint line can put the survivor at $145,000 into the second single tier, which is about $2,900 a year in Medicare surcharge on income that cost them nothing as a couple. When heirs inherit a traditional account, the SECURE Act gives them ten years to drain it, typically during their own peak-earning years. Both are dated, knowable events. Planning treats them as future tax rates you can arbitrage against today's.

To size any of this, the inputs are: current traditional, Roth, and taxable balances; the household's ages and a realistic year one spouse files single; projected income against the IRMAA tiers; the state's treatment of retirement income; the heirs' likely brackets in their ten-year window; and an assumption about future statutory rates, which is now a question of legislative risk rather than a scheduled reversion. With those inputs you can project lifetime-plus-heirs tax under "do nothing" against "convert deliberately in the low years." The difference between those two numbers is the money on the table.

That same projection answers the Roth question directly. A conversion makes sense when the rate you pay today is below the blended rate those dollars would face later, counting the taxes I flagged earlier and the heir's bracket, not just your current bracket. That is the economic side, and it maps directly to the NPV calculation in the second FPA paper you flagged. The utility side, what those particular dollars are worth to the household at the moment of conversion, is what the model cannot see. The decision lives where the two meet.

One honest caveat on "the rate those dollars would face later." That rate rests on an assumption about future statutory brackets, and it is worth being precise about what that assumption is now. The old urgency, convert before the 2026 sunset raises your rates, no longer applies: the One Big Beautiful Bill Act made the current brackets permanent, so there is no scheduled reversion. What is left is ordinary legislative risk, which cuts both ways, plus a few genuinely temporary provisions like the new senior deduction. If anything that sharpens the point. With the sunset fear removed, the case for converting rests on the household's own situation, the rising RMD, the halved survivor threshold, the heir's bracket, not on a forecast of Washington. The model is not a black box. Change the rate schedule and the conclusion can flip, which is exactly why you run the numbers rather than lean on a rule of thumb.

If it would be useful, I can run a before-and-after projection for a representative household and share it alongside the analysis.

John
